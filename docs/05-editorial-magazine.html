Against the Attention Economy's Last Defence

For fifteen years we have been told that distraction is a design problem. Perhaps it's a political one.

By Tobias Wren  ·  June 18, 2026  ·  18 min read

The discourse around attention has a telling asymmetry. We speak constantly of individuals who cannot focus, but rarely of the institutions that profit from their fragmentation. We treat distraction as a failure of personal willpower — a kind of cognitive weakness to be overcome through meditation apps, screen-time limits, and the rituals of digital minimalism.

This framing is not accidental. It serves the interests of companies whose revenue depends on the continuous capture of human attention. To frame the problem as personal is to absolve the system. And the system, as several economists have recently argued, is not a byproduct of market forces but a deliberate construction.

The question isn't whether you can resist your phone. The question is whether resistance itself should be necessary.

Consider the basic architecture of a social media feed. It is not, as is sometimes claimed, a neutral aggregation of information. It is an engineered environment, optimised by hundreds of engineers and trillions of data points, for a single metric: time on platform. Everything else — connection, information, community — is instrumentalised in service of that figure.
